1.3 Headline findings

Highest-rated ownerships

The highest ownership-benefit scores are Brentford (90) and Brighton & Hove Albion (88). Both are rated primarily for repeatable institutional models, sporting output above resource level, coherent football operations and long-term stability rather than for absolute spending power. Liverpool, AFC Bournemouth, Arsenal and Manchester City form the next group, all above 80.

Lowest-rated ownerships

The lowest scores are Burnley (36) and Wolverhampton Wanderers (38), followed by West Ham United, Tottenham Hotspur and Chelsea. Their weaknesses differ: Burnley’s assessment is dominated by leveraged acquisition risk and unstable league status; Wolves by strategic decline; Tottenham by failure to convert exceptional infrastructure into football performance; Chelsea by inefficient capital deployment and governance churn; and West Ham by deteriorating performance and unsettled control.

Scale-adjusted ownership value

The largest contrast between league position and ownership score appears at Brentford, Brighton and Bournemouth, whose ownership models rate considerably better than club scale alone would imply. Manchester United, Nottingham Forest, Chelsea, Tottenham and West Ham rate materially below what their brand, resources or occasional peak seasons might suggest. Fulham, Aston Villa, Leeds, Everton, Sunderland and Newcastle generally rate as beneficial but with important dependencies or financial-regulatory risks.

Interpretation caution

Small differences should not be over-read. The ranking is an evidence-weighted judgement rather than a mathematically exact measure, particularly where clubs have very similar scores.

2. Method and limitations

2.1 What the report assesses

This report assesses how beneficial each club’s current controlling ownership or effective control regime has been to the football club. It does not treat personal wealth, popularity, transfer expenditure, public displays of passion or raw league position as sufficient evidence of good ownership. The assessment instead covers sporting value relative to resources, governance, financial stewardship, capital commitment, football operations, infrastructure and revenue development, stability and adaptability, and stewardship of the club and its stakeholders.

2.2 Assessment window and evidence hierarchy

The assessment window is the five most recently completed seasons ending with 2025–26, shortened where the current owner or effective control regime has been in place for less time. The evidence hierarchy prioritises company filings, audited accounts and regulatory records, followed by official club statements, attributable executive evidence, high-quality journalism and specialist football-finance analysis.

2.3 Scoring and confidence

Each attribute is scored from 0 to 5. The Ownership Benefit Score is the weighted total converted to a score out of 100. Evidence confidence is reported separately and is not multiplied into the performance score. Confidence reflects source authority, period coverage, consistency, recency/comparability and the strength of ownership attribution.

2.4 Limits on comparison and attribution

Cross-club comparisons are useful but should not be read as mathematically exact, particularly where only a few points separate clubs.

Audited accounts also lag the completed 2025–26 season, private shareholder agreements are often incomplete, and ownership effects cannot be perfectly separated from executives, coaches, inherited squads, injuries, market conditions or chance. The Crystal Palace and West Ham assessments require extra caution because their effective control arrangements were especially recent or unsettled at the research date.

Wolves’ governance has been too reactive. Official club material shows repeated football-leadership restructuring, the late 2025 move away from Jeff Shi’s decade-long operational chairmanship, and a leadership reset around Nathan Shi, Matt Wild, Matt Jackson and others. The proper reading is that Fosun eventually recognised the need for change, which is a point in its favour. 3. Financial stewardship

Score: 2.4 / 5.0

Evidence confidence: 4.3 / 5.0

Confidence components: Authority 4.5 · Coverage 4.0 · Consistency 4.5 · Recency/comparability 4.5 · Ownership attribution 4.0

Wolves’ latest official position is more resilient than their league finish suggests: the club disclosed a £14.3m loss for 2023/24 and £15.3m for the 13-month 2024/25 period, supported by strong player-sale profits and some payroll control. The problem is quality, not mere survival.

The accounts and specialist analyses make clear that Wolves’ business model has become too dependent on trading out good players to fund itself, while operating costs remain structurally high relative to turnover. So Fosun deserve credit for avoiding a regulatory or solvency disaster, but not for creating a compelling or resilient model.

6. Evidence gaps and further research

The main common evidence gaps are the absence of audited 2025–26 accounts, incomplete visibility into private shareholder agreements and internal decision rights, uncertainty over the exact mix and terms of equity, shareholder loans and guarantees at several clubs, and limited public evidence on internal recruitment, medical, performance and staff-culture processes. Future revision would benefit most from a single recalibration pass across all 20 clubs once 2025–26 accounts are filed and the unresolved regulatory or control questions at Manchester City, Crystal Palace and West Ham are clearer.
